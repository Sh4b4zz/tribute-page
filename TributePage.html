Gil Scott-Heron

forgotten poet of the 20th Century

Gil Scott heron, activist, musician and poet

Here are some interesting facts about Gil Scott-Heron:


  • Born April 1, 1949 in Chicago, Illinois.

  • an American soul and jazz poet, musician, and author, known primarily for his work as a spoken-word performer in the 1970s and 1980s.

  • his lyrics dealt with social and political issues of the time.

  • His music influenced and foreshadowed later African-American music genres such as hip hop and neo soul.

  • He is considered by many to be the first rapper/MC ever.

  • He is included in the exhibits at the National Museum of African American History and Culture.

  • began his recording career in 1970 with the LP Small Talk at 125th and Lenox. The album's 14 tracks dealt with themes such as the superficiality of television and mass consumerism, the hypocrisy of some would-be black revolutionaries, and white middle-class ignorance of the difficulties faced by inner-city residents.

  • Some of his influences were Richie Havens, John Coltrane, Otis Redding, Jose Feliciano, Billie Holiday, Langston Hughes, Malcolm X, Huey Newton, Nina Simone, and long-time collaborator Brian Jackson.

  • He went on to release 13 more albums

  • He is perhaps best known for his spoken word song called "The Revolution Will Not Be Televised".

  • His music has been sampled by many other artists especially hip hop artists such as common and kanye west.

  • Regarding hip hop music in the 1990s, he said in an interview: "They need to study music. I played in several bands before I began my career as a poet. There's a big difference between putting words over some music, and blending those same words into the music. There's not a lot of humor. They use a lot of slang and colloquialisms, and you don't really see inside the person. Instead, you just get a lot of posturing."

  • He wrote 6 books.

  • had spent many years in and out of prison and battling addiction

  • HIV-positive for the last several years of his life.

  • remained active until his death, and in 2010 released his first new album in 16 years, entitled I'm New Here. A memoir he had been working on for years up to the time of his death.

  • died on the afternoon of May 27, 2011, at St. Luke's Hospital, New York City, after becoming ill upon returning from a European trip.
